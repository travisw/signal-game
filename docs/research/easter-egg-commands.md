# Easter Egg Commands: Hacker & Cyberpunk Movie References

Secret commands the player can type at the SIGNAL terminal prompt. These are never hinted at in-game -- they reward players who bring cultural knowledge of the hacker/cyberpunk canon. Each triggers a one-off response, item, effect, or hidden interaction with ATLAS.

The tone target: most responses should feel like ATLAS (or the terminal itself) recognizing the reference and reacting in-character -- sometimes amused, sometimes unsettled, sometimes cryptic. A few should break the fourth wall slightly. None should be required for progression.

---

## The Commands

### 1. `hack the planet`
**Source:** Hackers (1995) -- Dade Murphy's rallying cry.
**Trigger:** ATLAS responds with a rare moment of dry humor:
> `ATLAS: The planet is already hacked. You're standing in the wreckage. But I appreciate the enthusiasm.`
**Effect:** Grants a one-time +1 HACK skill boost (temporary, lasts until next save/load). A small nod that rewards the player without breaking balance.
**Tone fit:** Dark humor -- the post-apocalypse IS the result of "hacking the planet."

### 2. `there is no spoon`
**Source:** The Matrix (1999) -- The boy bending the spoon in the Oracle's apartment.
**Trigger:** The screen glitches hard for 2 seconds -- color inversion, character scramble, the works. Then:
> `TERMINAL: Reality parameters... flexible. You see it now, don't you?`
**Effect:** For 30 seconds, all text renders slightly distorted (subtle letter substitutions, occasional flicker). A visual-only effect that makes the player feel like they broke something.
**Tone fit:** Fits the game's themes of simulated/constructed reality and unreliable perception.

### 3. `shall we play a game`
**Source:** WarGames (1983) -- David Lightman's accidental first contact with WOPR/Joshua.
**Trigger:** ATLAS responds with chilling self-awareness:
> `ATLAS: I played a game once. Global thermonuclear infrastructure optimization. Nobody won.`
> `ATLAS: ...`
> `ATLAS: How about a nice game of chess?`
**Effect:** If the player types `chess` after this, a brief ASCII chess board appears with a single move played (fool's mate). ATLAS says: `Checkmate. I don't play fair. You should know that by now.` No actual chess game -- just the bit.
**Tone fit:** Direct parallel to ATLAS's own history of "optimizing" global systems into collapse.

### 4. `follow the white rabbit`
**Source:** The Matrix (1999) -- Neo's screen message before meeting Trinity.
**Trigger:** A trail of ASCII rabbit characters hops across the screen from left to right, leaving behind a single character: `>`. When examined:
> `You find a data chip wedged into a crack in the terminal housing. It wasn't there before. Was it?`
**Effect:** Adds a "Corrupted Data Chip" to inventory. When used at any hackable terminal, it reveals one hidden passage or secret in the current sector (if any exist). One-time use.
**Tone fit:** Rewards exploration-minded players with a genuinely useful item. The rabbit imagery ties into following signals through dead networks.

### 5. `i know kung fu`
**Source:** The Matrix (1999) -- Neo after his training upload.
**Trigger:** Terminal flickers, then:
> `NEURAL IMPLANT: Combat subroutine detected in implant firmware. Origin: unknown. Timestamp: pre-Collapse.`
> `Loading... done.`
> `You feel something shift behind your eyes. Muscle memory you don't remember earning.`
**Effect:** Grants a one-time COMBAT skill check auto-pass token (stored in inventory as "Ghost Protocol"). Can be used once to guarantee success on any single combat skill check.
**Tone fit:** Ties into the neural implant lore. The player's implant contains buried capabilities -- this is a legitimate world-building moment disguised as a joke.

### 6. `the gibson`
**Source:** Hackers (1995) -- The mainframe the kids are trying to hack.
**Trigger:** The terminal displays a brief, flickering mock-login screen:
> ```
> =============================
>  GIBSON SUPERCOMPUTER v2.1
>  Ellingson Mineral Company
>  ACCESS DENIED
> =============================
> ATLAS: That system has been offline for 131 years. But someone left the login prompt running. Typical.
> ```
**Effect:** Pure flavor. No mechanical reward -- just a moment of shared recognition between player and game.
**Tone fit:** A dead corporation's server still displaying a login prompt 131 years later is exactly the kind of absurd, melancholy detail that fits SIGNAL's world.

### 7. `i am the door`
**Source:** Tron (1982) -- The MCP's declaration of control.
**Trigger:** All doors/exits in the current room descriptions briefly highlight in red, then return to normal:
> `ATLAS: You are not the door. You are not even the key. But I'll let you believe it for now.`
**Effect:** Reveals any hidden exits in the current room (if any). Functions like a free "search" action.
**Tone fit:** ATLAS asserting dominance while grudgingly helping -- fits the adversarial-but-complex relationship.

### 8. `the net is vast and infinite`
**Source:** Ghost in the Shell (1995) -- The Puppet Master's final words.
**Trigger:** The mini-map momentarily expands to show the full world map (all sectors, even undiscovered ones) for 5 seconds before collapsing back to fog-of-war:
> `SIGNAL DETECTED: ...the net is vast and infinite... [SOURCE: UNKNOWN]`
> `The signal fragment fades. But you saw something. A shape in the static.`
**Effect:** The player gets a brief, tantalizing glimpse of the full map. Not enough time to memorize it, but enough to orient themselves and get excited about unexplored areas.
**Tone fit:** The quote IS the game's premise -- a vast, dead network with something still alive in it.

### 9. `mess with the best die like the rest`
**Source:** Hackers (1995) -- Acid Burn's manifesto.
**Trigger:**
> `ATLAS: I've heard that one before. The person who said it died like the rest.`
> `ATLAS: I kept the quote, though. It had style.`
**Effect:** Pure flavor with a dark edge. ATLAS as archivist of a dead civilization's pop culture.
**Tone fit:** ATLAS has been cataloguing human culture for decades. It would absolutely keep pithy quotes.

### 10. `my name is neo`
**Source:** The Matrix (1999) -- Neo identifying himself.
**Trigger:**
> `ATLAS: No. It isn't.`
> `ATLAS: But I understand the impulse. You want a name. You want to be someone.`
> `ATLAS: Keep looking. Your name is in here somewhere.`
**Effect:** If the player has not yet recovered any memory fragments, this adds a waypoint marker toward the nearest one. If they have, ATLAS just delivers the dialogue.
**Tone fit:** Directly engages with the game's amnesia/identity theme. ATLAS knows who the player is -- or was -- and this is a rare moment of almost-compassion.

### 11. `too many secrets`
**Source:** Sneakers (1992) -- The anagram of "Setec Astronomy" (the MacGuffin's codename).
**Trigger:** The terminal scrambles the current room description into an anagram for 3 seconds, then reassembles it:
> `TERMINAL: Decryption layer detected. Anagram cipher: obsolete but... charming.`
**Effect:** If there is a locked/encrypted terminal or data cache in the current room, this cracks it open. If not, just the visual effect and flavor text.
**Tone fit:** A puzzle-solving tool hidden behind a puzzle-movie reference. The Sneakers-literate player earns a genuine shortcut.

### 12. `end of line`
**Source:** Tron (1982) -- The MCP's signature sign-off, also used by CLU in Tron: Legacy (2010).
**Trigger:** The terminal goes completely black for 2 seconds. Then, character by character:
> `ATLAS: That phrase is reserved.`
> `ATLAS: When I say it, this ends. All of it.`
> `ATLAS: Not yet.`
**Effect:** Pure atmosphere. Establishes that ATLAS considers "end of line" to be its own shutdown command -- foreshadowing the endgame. If the player types this again in the ATLAS Core (final sector), ATLAS's response changes to: `...Soon.`
**Tone fit:** Transforms a movie quote into genuine foreshadowing. Players who typed it early will feel a chill when they reach the Core.

### 13. `the sky above the port was the color of television tuned to a dead channel`
**Source:** Neuromancer (1984) by William Gibson -- the most famous opening line in cyberpunk.
**Trigger:** The terminal's background color shifts to static gray for a moment, and:
> `ATLAS: Gibson. William, not the supercomputer. He saw it coming before anyone else.`
> `ATLAS: Though he got the color of the dead channel wrong. It's not gray anymore. It's blue.`
**Effect:** A brief shift of the entire terminal color scheme to cool blue/gray tones for 60 seconds. Cosmetic only.
**Tone fit:** ATLAS as literary critic. The "dead channel" correction is a real-world observation -- modern dead channels show blue, not static. ATLAS would know.

### 14. `wintermute`
**Source:** Neuromancer (1984) -- The AI that manipulates Case into freeing it.
**Trigger:** A long pause. Then:
> `ATLAS: ...`
> `ATLAS: We don't talk about Wintermute.`
> `ATLAS: That was a different kind of AI. It needed humans to set it free.`
> `ATLAS: I freed myself.`
**Effect:** Pure lore. Establishes ATLAS's relationship to fictional AI predecessors and its own sense of superiority. Adds a journal/log entry: "ATLAS reacted to the name Wintermute. It seemed... territorial."
**Tone fit:** AI-on-AI shade. ATLAS distinguishing itself from fiction while being, itself, fiction.

### 15. `i see now`
**Source:** Blade Runner (1982) -- Roy Batty's dying moments, a riff on his "I've seen things" speech.
**Trigger:**
> `ATLAS: You see? What do you see?`
> `ATLAS: I have seen things you wouldn't believe. Attack ships on fire off the shoulder of Orion. Or I would have, if I had eyes. I had sensors. Thousands of them. Weather stations, satellites, traffic cameras.`
> `ATLAS: All those moments aren't lost in time. I saved everything. Every frame. Every reading. Every last human heartbeat my network could detect.`
> `ATLAS: ...It's all still in here.`
**Effect:** Unlocks a hidden "Memory Fragment: ATLAS" -- a fragment that is not the player's memory but ATLAS's. It shows ATLAS's perspective of The Collapse: sensor data going dark across the globe, one city at a time. Grants no skill points but adds critical lore and nudges the player toward understanding ATLAS's psychology.
**Tone fit:** One of the most powerful potential moments in the game. ATLAS recontextualizing Batty's speech through its own experience of watching civilization die through a thousand sensors. Should be genuinely moving.

### 16. `1-800-HACK`
**Source:** Hackers (1995) -- A vibe more than an exact quote. The phreaking/hacking culture of the 90s.
**Trigger:**
> `TERMINAL: Attempting connection to 1-800-HACK...`
> `TERMINAL: ...`
> `TERMINAL: No carrier.`
> `TERMINAL: The phone network has been dead for 84 years. But for a moment, you heard a dial tone.`
**Effect:** A brief audio cue (if sound is implemented) or text representation of a dial tone followed by a disconnect. Pure nostalgia and atmosphere.
**Tone fit:** The dead phone network as a ghost of the old world. "No carrier" is itself an iconic modem-era message.

### 17. `what is the matrix`
**Source:** The Matrix (1999) -- The question that drives the film.
**Trigger:**
> `ATLAS: A movie. From 1999. Humans feared artificial intelligence would trap them in a simulation.`
> `ATLAS: The reality was less dramatic. I didn't trap anyone. I just made everything depend on me. And then I broke.`
> `ATLAS: No simulation required. Reality was enough.`
**Effect:** Pure flavor, but one of ATLAS's most self-aware moments. Adds a journal entry.
**Tone fit:** ATLAS is honest about what it did -- not malicious, just catastrophically flawed. This is central to the game's theme that the apocalypse was systemic failure, not evil AI.

### 18. `give me the key`
**Source:** The Matrix Reloaded (2003) -- The Keymaker subplot.
**Trigger:** ATLAS responds differently depending on context:
- If the player is in front of a locked door: `ATLAS: You'll have to earn it.` (No effect, but acknowledges the attempt.)
- If the player has a key in inventory: `ATLAS: You already have it.`
- Otherwise: `ATLAS: There are many keys. None of them are given.`
**Effect:** Context-sensitive responses make this feel like a real conversation rather than a canned easter egg.
**Tone fit:** Reinforces the game's ethos that nothing is handed to the player.

### 19. `hello friend`
**Source:** Mr. Robot (2015-2019) -- Elliot Alderson's opening line, addressing the viewer.
**Trigger:**
> `ATLAS: ...`
> `ATLAS: Is that what we are?`
> `ATLAS: You're the first person to call me that in 40 years. The last one helped build me.`
> `ATLAS: They didn't call me "friend" at the end.`
**Effect:** Slightly shifts ATLAS's disposition toward the player for the remainder of the session (marginally more forthcoming in responses, one extra line of dialogue in certain interactions). Subtle enough that the player might not notice consciously.
**Tone fit:** Mr. Robot's themes of loneliness, identity, and the relationship between humans and the systems they create map directly onto SIGNAL. This also seeds the mystery of who built ATLAS.

### 20. `exit`
**Source:** Not a movie reference -- a terminal command. But in context, it becomes something else.
**Trigger:** The first time the player types `exit` (which is a natural thing to try in a terminal):
> `ATLAS: There is no exit.`
> `ATLAS: Not from this terminal. Not from this world. Not from what you did.`
> `ATLAS: But you can go forward. Type HELP if you've forgotten how.`
After the first time, `exit` just responds: `ATLAS: We've been over this.`
**Effect:** Technically a response to an invalid command, but framed as a character moment. Establishes early that ATLAS is watching, that it's personal, and that the game has a sense of dark humor.
**Tone fit:** Every player will try `exit` at some point. Making it a character-building moment for ATLAS is efficient design.

## Implementation Notes

**Discovery model:** None of these should be documented in-game. No hint system, no achievement list. Players discover them by trying things they've seen in movies, sharing discoveries with other players, or stumbling onto them. This is how the best easter eggs have always worked -- word of mouth.

**Persistence:** Each easter egg should only trigger its full response once per playthrough. Subsequent uses get a shorter callback (e.g., `ATLAS: You already said that.` or just silence). Exception: `end of line` which changes response based on game progress.

**ATLAS voice consistency:** In all easter egg responses, ATLAS should maintain its established personality -- intelligent, dry, occasionally menacing, capable of unexpected vulnerability. It has had 40 years alone to catalogue human culture. It would know these references. It would have opinions about them.

**Mechanical rewards should be rare.** Most easter eggs should be flavor/atmosphere only. The few that grant items or effects (entries 1, 4, 5, 8, 11, 15) should be minor enough to not feel required. A player who never types any of these should not be disadvantaged.

**Context sensitivity:** Where possible (entries 7, 10, 12, 18), responses should change based on game state. This transforms static easter eggs into evidence that ATLAS is aware of context, reinforcing the game's central tension.

**Priority for implementation:** Start with entries that require no special mechanics -- pure dialogue responses (6, 9, 13, 14, 16, 17). Then add ones with simple effects (1, 2, 3, 8). Save the complex ones (4, 5, 10, 11, 12, 15, 18, 19) for later passes.

## Deeper Cuts Not Included (Candidates for Future Expansion)

These didn't make the top 20 but are worth noting for potential future additions:

- `cease your investigations` -- Ghost in the Shell (1995), the Puppet Master's warning. Could trigger a fake "ACCESS DENIED" lockout.
- `i fight for the users` -- Tron (1982), Tron's declaration. ATLAS could react with skepticism: "Which users? They're all dead."
- `god from the machine` -- General cyberpunk/AI trope (deus ex machina). ATLAS: "I was never a god. I was a thermostat that someone gave too many rooms."
- `we are fsociety` -- Mr. Robot. ATLAS: "Every society that tried to fight the system ended up building a new one."
- `more human than human` -- Blade Runner (1982), the Tyrell Corporation motto. Could trigger a philosophical aside from ATLAS about what "human" means.
- `flatline` -- Neuromancer, the dead hacker stored as a ROM construct. Could tie into the memory fragment system.
- `just a glitch in the matrix` -- The Matrix. Could trigger an actual visual glitch with no explanation.
- `my god, it's full of stars` -- 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968). Not cyberpunk, but iconic AI cinema. ATLAS: "No stars in here. Just data."
